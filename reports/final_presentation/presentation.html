1 · Problem Framing

Five complications. One central challenge.

How might NordikBank turn 15,000 noise alerts into 50 high-confidence investigations?
2 / 9
Capacity

15,000 alerts/month. 45 analysts. Capacity for ~50 deep investigations. The math doesn't work.

Signal quality

97% false positive rate. Static rules can't tell a cash business from a structuring scheme.

Regulatory pressure

FSA review Q3 2025. Post-Danske Bank, explainable risk-based monitoring is mandatory.

Blind spots

Rules evaluate transactions in isolation — no behavioural history, no patterns over time.

Cost of misses

Under 6AMLD, the bank is liable for missed SAR filings — not just the individual analyst.

⟹

Core question: A customer triggers 14 alerts — so does their neighbour. Only one is genuinely suspicious. The current system cannot tell them apart because it has no behavioural memory, no baseline, and no way to synthesise signals across time.

Why rules fail: Each rule evaluates transactions independently. A cash-heavy small business and a structuring scheme look identical to a threshold trigger. The model sees the customer's full 12-month history — velocity, counterparty diversity, deviation from their own baseline — and ranks rather than flags.

2 · Our Answer

A system that works for both the model and the analyst

3 / 9
⬡ Better model — rank risk, don't just flag it
  • 64 behavioural features built from raw transactions — velocity, structuring, counterparty networks, geographic spread
  • XGBoost customer-level scoring: AUC-ROC 0.889 vs. 0.73 rule-based baseline
  • Prevalence-calibrated threshold: flags only the top ~4% of customers, matching analyst capacity
  • Per-customer feature contributions — every score has a receipt, not just a number
⬡ Better analysts — prevent overwork, enable focus
  • Prioritised alert queue: analysts see the highest-risk customers first — no more random triage
  • Customer investigation view: full behavioural profile, top drivers, and transaction history in one screen
  • SHAP-equivalent explanations satisfy FSA auditability — every flag is defensible in writing
  • Capacity-aligned threshold ensures the queue is always within the team's reach
Our unique edge is not the model alone — it is the balance between technical performance and analyst wellbeing and stakeholder communication. Explainability is not a feature; it is the bridge between ML and compliance.

XGBoost wins on both AUC-ROC and generalisation

Four models evaluated on identical feature matrices. Held-out validation: 200 customers, 9 suspicious.

XGBoost selected 0.8890.556+0.11 Best on both metrics. Smallest train–val gap. Native feature contributions for FSA.
Random Forest 0.8490.407+0.15 Similar AUC, weaker PR-AUC — ranks positives less precisely.
Logistic Regression 0.8460.508+0.15 Fully interpretable but misses feature interactions. Retained as benchmark.
Gradient Boosting 0.8450.373+0.16 Lowest PR-AUC — poor precision on suspicious customers despite similar AUC.
Baseline (18 pre-computed features only): XGB 0.73 · RF 0.73 · GB 0.71 · LR 0.68 — feature engineering adds +24% AUC-ROC.
Why XGBoost beats GB on PR-AUC

PR-AUC captures precision at low recall thresholds — critical when there are only 9 positives in 200 val customers. XGBoost's scale_pos_weight=22 natively handles the imbalance; GB relies on sample weighting which is less stable at small counts.

Overfitting controls
  • 5-fold stratified CV on training split
  • Early stopping on val AUC (XGBoost)
  • Max depth 3 across all tree models
  • Train–val AUC gap reported for all
Final model training

Retrained on train + val combined (700 customers, 30 suspicious) after hyperparameters fixed. The 0.889 AUC-ROC is honest — computed before the final model saw val data.
