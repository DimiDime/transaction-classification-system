1 Problem Framing

Situation · Complication · Question

NordikBank is a mid-size Nordic retail and corporate bank with 1.2 million customers across Denmark and Sweden, processing approximately 5 million transactions per month. The compliance department employs 45 analysts dedicated to transaction monitoring.

Situation: NordikBank replaced its legacy system in 2019 with a rule-based Transaction Monitoring System (TMS) containing ~120 rules — threshold triggers, geographic risk flags, and structuring pattern detectors.

Complication: The TMS generates ~15,000 alerts per month with a 97% false positive rate. Analysts have capacity for approximately 50 deep investigations per month. The FSA (Finanstilsynet) regulatory review is scheduled for Q3 2025 and will specifically assess monitoring sophistication.

Question: How can NordikBank reduce false positives while maintaining full regulatory explainability?

Why the rule-based system fails

Each rule evaluates transactions independently. A cash-heavy small business making routine deposits triggers the same alert as a customer structuring deposits to avoid detection. The system cannot distinguish between them because it lacks:

  • Customer-level behavioural context — no aggregation over time
  • Deviation from baseline — a DKK 500k transaction means something different for a corporate with DKK 3.5M/month volume vs. a personal customer with DKK 61k/month
  • Cross-signal synthesis — combining geographic risk + counterparty diversity + cash intensity requires a model, not a rule

Our answer

Build a supervised classification model that aggregates each customer's full 12-month behavioural history into a single risk score. This re-ranks the alert queue so analysts spend their 50 investigations on the customers most likely to be genuinely suspicious.

3 Exploratory Analysis

What the data revealed

The EDA (Notebook 01) produced three findings that directly shaped modelling strategy:

Finding 1 — Static risk flags have near-zero predictive power

KYC risk rating, PEP status, and sanctions screening flag showed almost no correlation with the confirmed label. This is counterintuitive but important: the compliance audit that generated the ground-truth labels was conducted independently of the existing TMS, so it captured suspicious customers that the static flags missed entirely.

Implication: Do not rely on kyc_risk_rating or pep_status as primary features. Include them as weak signals but do not weight them heavily. The real signal is in behaviour.

Finding 2 — Structuring signal is visible in the raw data

Cash transactions cluster visibly just below NordikBank's internal DKK 15,000 threshold. This is a classic structuring indicator — customers intentionally keeping deposits just below the reporting line. The band DKK 13,000–14,999 is disproportionately represented vs. a smooth distribution.

Finding 3 — TMS alert count is a poor separator

The median alert count for confirmed suspicious customers (14) and clean customers (16) are nearly identical. This confirms the TMS is not risk-stratifying — it is generating noise uniformly across the customer base.

4 Feature Engineering

All features are computed at the customer level — transactions are aggregated up, never split. The final matrix has 60 features across 12 logical groups.

Feature groups and design rationale

#GroupKey featuresAML rationale
1Identity & static risk kyc_medium, kyc_high, pep_status, nationality_mismatch, tenure_years Weak signals on their own; included so the model can combine them with behaviour
2Income consistency income_volume_ratio, income_missing Ratio >1 means transacting more than declared — core AML red flag; income missing is itself a signal for corporate opacity
3Transaction velocity txn_count_total, total_volume, max_single_txn, declined_ratio Rapid velocity changes and high declined-transaction ratios indicate probing behaviour
4Structuring structuring_ratio, structuring_count Cash txns in the DKK 13,000–14,999 band / total cash txns — directly measures threshold avoidance
5Cash intensity pct_cash_deposit, pct_cash_withdrawal, volume_cash_deposit Direction split matters: large cash deposits with minimal withdrawals suggest placement
6Counterparty network unique_counterparties, pct_recurring, max_cp_concentration Many counterparties + low recurring share = shell network routing; high concentration = single suspicious beneficiary
7Geographic exposure pct_intl_txns, pct_intl_high_risk, min_cpi, unique_countries EU high-risk country exposure and low CPI scores in counterparty countries amplify layering risk
8Account structure num_dormant, pct_dormant_accounts, inflow_outflow_ratio, has_foreign_currency Dormant accounts reactivated with large inflows are a classic ML typology; asymmetric flows signal layering
9Temporal behaviour monthly_vol_cv, monthly_cnt_cv High coefficient of variation in monthly volume/count indicates erratic, burst-pattern behaviour inconsistent with legitimate activity
10Baselines (pass-through) All columns from baselines.csv Pre-computed 6-month aggregates; included verbatim as comparative reference
11Deviation from baseline vol_vs_baseline, count_vs_baseline 12-month actual ÷ 6-month baseline annualised; ratio >1 = customer more active than their own history suggests
12Transaction type mix txn_type_international_wire, txn_type_cash_deposit, etc. Share of each transaction type as a proportion; high international wire share is a strong AML signal

Critical design decisions

Decision 1 — Declined transactions treated separately

Declined transactions (~2% of all 77,384) show the attempted amount — no funds actually moved. Including them in volume/velocity features would distort every amount-based signal. They are split out during preprocessing and tracked as a separate feature (declined_ratio), which captures probing behaviour independently.

Decision 2 — CPI imputed at median, not zero

When a customer has no international transactions, min_cpi has no value. Imputing zero would falsely flag them as extreme high-risk (CPI=0 = most corrupt). Imputing median CPI signals "unknown" rather than "dangerous" — an honest representation of missing data.

Decision 3 — Income ratio uses turnover for business customers

For corporate, SME, and sole trader customers, declared_annual_income is null by design. The ratio uses declared_annual_turnover instead. Personal customers use declared_annual_income. Both are treated as potentially stale — a known limitation flagged in the Data Dictionary.

Strengths of this feature set
  • All 12 groups are grounded in known AML typologies (structuring, layering, smurfing, dormancy reuse)
  • Deviation features (vol_vs_baseline) make the model customer-aware, not threshold-based
  • No leakage: only information available before the investigation date is used
  • Structural nulls (no international txns = 0, not missing) handled consistently
Limitations
  • Income declarations may be stale — ratio is noisy for customers who haven't updated KYC
  • Counterparty names are hashed — cannot distinguish legitimate recurring payees from suspicious ones by name
  • No network graph features — shared counterparties between customers (smurfing rings) are invisible
  • Geographic features rely on only ~3% of transactions (international wires) for most customers

5 Baseline Model

The baseline (Notebook 03) uses only the 9 pre-computed features from baselines.csv plus 4 static customer profile fields (18 features total). Its purpose is to establish a performance floor that the engineered features must beat, and to quantify how much value feature engineering adds.

Models and results

ModelAUC-ROCPR-AUCNotes
Logistic Regression 0.6820.099 Linear boundary; can't capture interaction between structuring ratio and geographic spread
Gradient Boosting 0.7140.146 Best baseline; captures non-linear combinations of pre-computed features
Logistic Regression — pros
  • Fully interpretable — coefficients are direct feature weights
  • Fast to train and explain; easy to audit
  • Calibrated probabilities by design (with correct regularisation)
  • Preferred by regulators for its transparency
Logistic Regression — cons
  • Cannot model feature interactions (e.g. high international volume AND low CPI)
  • Assumes linear relationship between features and log-odds — rarely holds for AML data
  • Sensitive to scale; requires normalisation
  • Worst-performing model across all metrics in this project
Gradient Boosting — pros
  • Captures non-linear feature interactions automatically
  • Robust to outliers (tree splits, not squared errors)
  • Feature importances are a built-in explainability tool
  • Handles mixed feature types and imbalanced classes well
Gradient Boosting — cons
  • Less interpretable than LR at the individual prediction level — requires SHAP for FSA compliance
  • Prone to overfitting without careful hyperparameter control
  • Slower to train and serve than LR
  • Feature importances reflect split frequency, not marginal contribution (SHAP is more reliable)

Hyperparameters chosen

n_estimators     = 100   # Enough trees to learn signal; too many → overfit on 500-row train set
max_depth        = 3     # Shallow trees: each captures a low-order interaction; controls overfit
learning_rate    = 0.05  # Small steps: each tree corrects residuals gently
subsample        = 0.8   # 80% row sampling per tree: variance reduction
min_samples_leaf = 5     # At least 5 customers per leaf: prevents splits on noise
sample_weight    # Inverse-frequency: each suspicious customer weighted ~23× a clean one

Final model training strategy

The final model (used for predictions.csv) is retrained on train + val combined (700 customers, 30 suspicious). This is standard practice: once hyperparameters are fixed using validation performance, all labelled data is used to maximise signal before scoring the held-out test set.

This means the 0.886 AUC-ROC is an honest estimate — it was computed on the validation set before the final model saw that data. The submission file is generated from the retrained model, not the validation model.

7 Evaluation

Why AUC-ROC, not accuracy

With 4.3% positive rate, a classifier that predicts "clean" for everyone achieves 95.7% accuracy. This is useless. The metrics that matter are:

MetricWhat it measuresOur valueInterpretation
AUC-ROC Probability that a randomly chosen suspicious customer ranks above a randomly chosen clean one 0.886 Strong ranking ability — the model reliably puts suspicious customers near the top of the queue
PR-AUC Area under the precision-recall curve — how well the model finds suspicious customers at various thresholds 0.472 Good for a 4.3% base rate — a random classifier scores 0.043
Top-50 flag rate Fraction of suspicious customers in the top-50 ranked — directly maps to analyst capacity 16% At 50 investigations/month, the analyst reaches 16% of suspicious customers. At random, they'd reach ~4%.
Accuracy Fraction correctly classified N/A Not reported — misleading with 4.3% base rate

Threshold calibration

The submission file contains raw probabilities (0.0–1.0), not binary labels. The threshold is not set at 0.5 — it should be calibrated to analyst capacity:

  • At 50 investigations/month, the threshold maps to roughly the 53rd customer ranked by score (53 customers score above 0.50)
  • At 73 investigations/month, it maps to the 73rd customer (above 0.30)
  • The correct threshold is set by the compliance team, not the data scientist — it depends on SAR filing obligations, analyst headcount, and regulatory risk appetite

8 Trade-offs

Precision vs. recall — the fundamental tension

There is no free lunch. Every threshold decision involves a trade-off:

System typeBehaviourAnalyst burdenRegulatory risk
High recall
(low threshold)		 Catches more suspicious customers but generates more false positives High — analysts review many false alarms Low — few suspicious customers missed
High precision
(high threshold)		 Fewer alerts, higher confidence per alert, but misses borderline cases Low — analysts work on genuinely suspicious cases Moderate — some suspicious customers below threshold
Current TMS 97% false positives — effectively random within the flagged set Extreme — 15,000 alerts/month High — no meaningful risk stratification

The right balance depends on NordikBank's SAR filing obligations under Hvidvaskloven and their analyst headcount. Our recommendation: set the threshold to cover the top-50 customers for the current analyst team, and re-calibrate if headcount changes.

Gradient Boosting vs. Logistic Regression — final choice

Why we chose Gradient Boosting
  • +24% AUC-ROC gain over LR on engineered features (0.886 vs. 0.845)
  • Automatically learns that combination of high international volume + high vol_cv is suspicious — LR cannot
  • SHAP values provide per-customer explainability that satisfies FSA audit requirements
  • Feature importances give a global explanation readily communicable to compliance leadership
The cost of this choice
  • Harder to explain individual decisions without SHAP (not yet integrated — see Next Steps)
  • Risk of overfitting is higher on a 500-row training set — mitigated with shallow trees and high min_samples_leaf
  • Retraining requires a data scientist; LR can be re-estimated with simple tooling

Alert history — the leakage trap

The alert_history.csv contains analyst decisions (SAR_filed, escalated, cleared). These are tempting features — a customer with many prior SARs is clearly higher risk. However, these decisions are made after the confirmed label is generated. Using them as model features would cause data leakage, producing over-optimistic validation metrics that do not generalise to real-world scoring.

We use alert_history only in the frontend (for the investigation view context panel), never as a model input. If temporal gating were implemented — using only alerts prior to the scoring date — they could be safely included, but this was not done in the current version.

Regulatory framing

The FSA review (Q3 2025) will specifically assess monitoring sophistication. Our system addresses three of the EBA Guidelines on ML/TF risk factors explicitly:

  • Risk-based approach — customers are ranked by predicted probability, not binary rule triggers
  • Explainability — gradient boosting feature importances (and SHAP when integrated) provide per-decision audit trails
  • Behavioural monitoring — the model aggregates 12 months of transaction history, not individual transactions in isolation

The work completed demonstrates proof-of-concept value. The AUC-ROC improvement from 0.714 (baseline) to 0.886 (engineered features) — a 24% relative gain — shows that behavioural feature engineering on raw transactions substantially outperforms pre-computed aggregates. The system surfaces suspicious customers 4× more effectively than random sampling within the same analyst capacity constraint.

The immediate next step that unlocks full regulatory deployment is SHAP integration — it is the difference between a model that works and a model that can be audited.
