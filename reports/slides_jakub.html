The system
Rule-based TMS introduced in 2019 after Nordic AML scandals. Static thresholds — cash limits, geographic flags, structuring patterns — applied transaction by transaction.
The analysts
Each investigation takes 20–45 min. With current capacity, analysts can conduct ~50 deep-dive investigations per month before hitting their limit.
The regulator
Finanstilsynet (FSA) review in Q3 2025. Following Danske Bank and Swedbank, risk-based and explainable monitoring is the new bar — not a nice-to-have.

Signal quality
Static rules cannot tell a cash-heavy business from a structuring scheme — both trip the same alert. 97% of analyst time is spent clearing noise.
Capacity gap
15,000 alerts generated monthly. With capacity for only 50 deep investigations, over 99% of the queue goes uninvestigated each month.
Regulatory pressure
Post-Danske Bank and Swedbank, the FSA expects risk-based, explainable monitoring. Static rule sets will not pass the Q3 '25 review.
Blind spots
Each rule sees one transaction at a time. Behavioural history, cross-account patterns, and change over time are invisible — layering goes undetected.
Cost of misses
Under 6AMLD, the bank — not the analyst — is liable for missed SAR filings. A false negative is not just a miss; it is a legal exposure.

Connect to live transaction feed
Replace the CSV snapshot with a real-time DB connection — nightly batch scoring or streaming via Kafka.
2
Analyst feedback loop
Capture SAR / clear decisions in the workbench and use them to retrain the model quarterly — the system learns from investigator judgement.
3
Drift monitoring & model governance
Track score distribution and feature drift over time. Alert the team when the model needs revalidation — required for FSA model risk management.
4
Expand to corporate customers
Current model is tuned for retail. Corporate AML patterns differ — higher volumes, more complex ownership structures — warranting a separate model.
5
Network graph for counterparty risk
Extend features to map counterparty networks across customers — detecting shell company clusters and multi-hop layering invisible to per-customer scoring.
Modelling
XGBoost with engineered features delivers an AUC-ROC of 0.89 — 22% above the rule-based baseline
Four models evaluated
XGBoost Selected
  • Best val AUC-ROC and best PR-AUC — wins on both metrics
  • Smallest train–val gap (+0.11) — most generalisable
  • Native per-customer explanations satisfy FSA auditability
Random Forest — dropped
  • Similar AUC to LR/GB, no meaningful gain over XGBoost
  • Weaker PR-AUC — ranks the 9 val positives less precisely
Logistic Regression — dropped
  • Lowest AUC-ROC — linear model misses feature interactions
  • Retained as interpretability benchmark only
Gradient Boosting — dropped
  • Lowest PR-AUC (0.37) — poor precision on suspicious customers
  • No native imbalance parameter; sample weighting less effective
